Every year as filing season opens, fraudsters ramp up their activity. The 2026 tax season is no different. Fake SARS SMS messages, phishing emails with convincing logos, fraudulent QR codes, and impersonation calls are already circulating ahead of the 1 July opening. Knowing how to spot a fake, and what to do when you receive one, could save you significant money and legal trouble.

## How SARS Tax Scams Work in 2026

The most common scams target taxpayers in three ways:

**Fake refund notifications**: A message claims SARS has approved a tax refund and asks you to click a link or scan a QR code to "confirm your banking details." The link leads to a convincing copy of the eFiling login page. Once you enter your credentials, fraudsters take control of your eFiling profile, redirect your refund to a different bank account, and lock you out.

**Fake audit or penalty notices**: A message claims SARS has opened a compliance case or is about to issue a penalty. It creates urgency and asks you to click a link or call a number to "resolve" the matter. The contact details belong to fraudsters, not SARS.

**Impersonation calls**: Someone calls claiming to be a SARS official and asks for your tax reference number, ID number, or banking details to "verify your account." SARS does call taxpayers but will never ask for banking details over the phone.

## What SARS Will Never Do

This is the most important rule: **SARS will never ask for your banking details via SMS, email, or unsolicited phone call.** SARS will also never ask you to click a link in an SMS or email to update your bank account. All banking detail changes on eFiling must be made while you are logged in directly at sars.gov.za.

Every genuine SARS communication will reference your tax reference number. If a message does not include your tax reference number or references a different number, treat it as suspicious.

## How to Verify Any SARS Communication

If you receive a message claiming to be from SARS and you are unsure whether it is genuine, do the following:

1. Do not click any link in the message
2. Open a new browser tab and navigate directly to sars.gov.za
3. Log in to your eFiling profile and check your correspondence under the Notices section
4. If the matter referenced in the message does not appear in your eFiling profile, the message is fake
5. Use the SARS Online Query System (SOQS) at sars.gov.za to check your account status independently

If you want to call SARS to verify, use only the published number: 0800 00 7277. Do not call any number provided in a suspicious message.

## What to Do If You Receive a Scam Message

1. Do not click the link, do not scan the QR code, do not reply, and do not call the number in the message
2. Forward the message or a screenshot to phishing@sars.gov.za
3. If you already clicked a link or entered your eFiling credentials, change your eFiling password immediately by logging in at sars.gov.za, then call SARS on 0800 00 7277 to flag the compromise and protect your account
4. Report the incident to the South African Police Service if you have suffered a financial loss

## Why This Matters for Your Business

Staff receiving payroll-related SARS notifications are frequent targets. Employees who receive IRP5-related messages, UIF correspondence, or EMP201 notifications are often not expecting to receive SARS communication and may be more likely to click a suspicious link without thinking.

If you employ staff, brief them before filing season opens. A simple message reminding your team that SARS will never ask for banking details via SMS or email, and that any SARS-related queries should be escalated to your accountant, significantly reduces the risk.

Business owners who use eFiling directly should also enable two-factor authentication on their eFiling profile if they have not already done so. This adds an additional layer of protection even if credentials are compromised.
